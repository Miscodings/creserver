Custom Armors

Custom armors are interesting because while there are multiple ways to add them, the method you must choose depends entirely on your server version. As recent Minecraft updates have become more and more data-driven, the more updated your server is, the less restrictions you have.

Armor Trims (1.20-1.21.1):

When using armor trims, you'll want to use CHAINMAIL armor as your base item. Also note that this does require a custom datapack (Nexo generates one for you), so you'll need to restart your server every time you add or remove a set.

Here's An Example:

Nexo/items/sorcerer_armor.yml

sorcerer_chestplate:
  itemname: "Sorcerer's Robe"
  material: CHAINMAIL_CHESTPLATE
  Pack:
    parent_model: "item/generated"
    texture: default/armors/sorcerer_chestplate
  trim_pattern: nexo:sorcerer

- `sorcerer_chestplate` is the ID of the item. Every item has its own, unique ID.

- The base item, or `material:` that this axe is using is a Chainmail Chestplate.

- The texture of this item is located in `assets/minecraft/default/armors/sorcerer_chestplate`.

- Your armor textures must be named in THIS format:[armorname]_armor_layer_1/2

- For my example, this would be `sorcerer_armor_layer_1` and `sorcerer_armor_layer_2`.

Components (1.21.2+) {THE BETTER METHOD!}

This is just the better option. No limits with this one. Any armor can be any item. Your armor could be PAPER if you wanted it to be. No downsides. Also, you can use this to make custom Wolf, Horse, Llama armor.

Here's An Example:

Nexo/items/sorcerer_armor.yml

sorcerer_chestplate:
  itemname: "Sorcerer's Robe"
  material: CHAINMAIL_CHESTPLATE
  Pack:
    texture: default/armors/sorcerer_chestplate
  Components:
    equippable:
      slot: CHEST
      model: nexo:sorcerer

Minor difference, but now your armors are data-driven and work much better.
